# 13. Privilege Inheritance Across Role Chains

Privilege inheritance across role chains allows higher-level roles to automatically acquire the permissions granted to lower-level roles within a hierarchical structure. 
This mechanism simplifies access management by ensuring that users assigned to a parent role seamlessly inherit all capabilities of its child roles without requiring redundant, direct grants.

# Key Points

* Role hierarchies are constructed by granting one role to another, creating a parent-child relationship where the parent inherits the child's privileges.
* Inheritance is transitive, meaning if Role A is granted to Role B, and Role B is granted to Role C, Role C inherits privileges from both Role A and Role B.
* Revoking a privilege from a lower-level role immediately removes that access from all higher-level roles in the chain, ensuring centralized security control.
* System-defined roles (like `SYSADMIN`) should typically sit at the top of custom role hierarchies to maintain comprehensive administrative oversight.
* Designing role chains based on business functions minimizes privilege bloat and adheres to the principle of least privilege.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> Always attach the highest-level custom roles in your hierarchy to the `SYSADMIN` role. Failing to do so creates "orphaned" roles, preventing system administrators from managing the objects created by those custom roles.

# Implementation

```sql
-- Step 1: Create the roles for the hierarchy
CREATE ROLE data_reader;
CREATE ROLE data_analyst;
CREATE ROLE data_scientist;

-- Step 2: Grant specific privileges to the lowest-level role
GRANT USAGE ON DATABASE analytics_db TO ROLE data_reader;
GRANT USAGE ON SCHEMA analytics_db.public TO ROLE data_reader;
GRANT SELECT ON ALL TABLES IN SCHEMA analytics_db.public TO ROLE data_reader;

-- Step 3: Grant additional privileges to the mid-level role
GRANT INSERT, UPDATE ON ALL TABLES IN SCHEMA analytics_db.public TO ROLE data_analyst;

-- Step 4: Build the role chain (Inheritance)
GRANT ROLE data_reader TO ROLE data_analyst;
GRANT ROLE data_analyst TO ROLE data_scientist;

-- Step 5: Assign the top-level role to a user and system admin
GRANT ROLE data_scientist TO USER alice;
GRANT ROLE data_scientist TO ROLE sysadmin;
```

# What does the Query do?

* **Step 1:** Creates three distinct roles (`data_reader`, `data_analyst`, and `data_scientist`) to represent different levels of access requirements within a data team.
* **Step 2:** Grants foundational read-only privileges on the `analytics_db` database, schema, and its tables directly to the `data_reader` role.
* **Step 3:** Grants write privileges (INSERT, UPDATE) directly to the `data_analyst` role, representing elevated access needs for data manipulation.
* **Step 4:** Constructs the inheritance chain by granting `data_reader` to `data_analyst`, and `data_analyst` to `data_scientist`. Because of transitive inheritance, `data_scientist` now possesses all read and write privileges from the lower roles.
* **Step 5:** Assigns the top-level `data_scientist` role to a specific user (`alice`) so she can utilize the full chain of permissions, and to the `sysadmin` role to ensure the system administrator maintains oversight of the entire custom hierarchy.

# When Should We Use This? 

* When designing scalable Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) models for large organizations where users need cumulative permissions based on their seniority or job function (e.g., a data scientist needs all analyst and reader permissions).
* To reduce administrative overhead and prevent errors by managing permissions at the lowest logical level, allowing access changes to automatically propagate up the chain without manual intervention.
